\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper introduced TransitDuet, a bi-level reinforcement learning framework that unifies bus timetable planning and real-time holding control within a single trainable system.
The upper policy learns a per-dispatch departure offset $\delta_t \in [-120, 120]$\,s, while the lower policy regulates individual holding actions at station arrivals---two tasks that have traditionally been addressed in isolation despite their tight operational coupling.

The technical core is a goal-conditioned cross-level coupling (Section~\ref{sec:tap}) in the spirit of HIRO~\citep{nachum2018data}: the upper outputs a per-dispatch target-headway shift, and the lower's Lagrangian cost is evaluated against the resulting goal headway, with no upper-advantage injection into the lower's transitions.
We compared this end-to-end against a literal cross-advantage augmentation following HAAR~\citep{li2019hierarchical} (with a PIPER-style reachability gate~\citep{singh2024piper}) and against a three-channel coupling that perturbs the launch time and supplies a hindsight reward credit; the goal-conditioned variant attained the best mean composite cost and the lowest seed variance among the three.
A target-policy-corrected lower SAC (TPC, Section~\ref{sec:tpc}) provides a complementary training-time correction that prevents residual covariate shift in the lower's training distribution.
Operational constraints are handled through complementary mechanisms: online gradient descent projection ($\thetavec$-OGD) reweights the upper-level fleet-overshoot penalty, while Lagrangian relaxation maintains headway regularity at the lower level.

Experiments on a stochastic bus corridor microsimulation with 22 stations and 264 daily trips show that, under a unified 300-episode training and held-out evaluation protocol, TransitDuet matches or marginally outperforms every baseline on every metric we report: composite cost $1.444$ vs $1.504$ for the strongest search baseline (CMA-ES, $-4.0\%$) and $1.544$ for the strongest hand-tuned fixed timetable ($-6.5\%$), with simultaneous improvements of $5.5$/$6.4$/$10.4\%$ on wait/CV/overshoot. The wait and CV margins against the search baselines fall within seed noise at $n{=}3$ seeds; we therefore report all distributions and avoid pairwise significance claims. Seed variance on composite cost is $1.7$--$2.5\times$ smaller than the search baselines, matching the deterministic Fixed timetable's variance despite being a learned controller. From a single training run the learned policy produces a Pareto frontier across nine fleet budgets ($\Nfleet \in [8, 16]$), removing the need for separate optimization per budget.
Architectural and training ablations identify the goal-conditioned coupling (Section~\ref{sec:tap}) as the dominant design choice within the bi-level framework, while holding-feedback and CS-BAPR are the two auxiliary components whose removal materially increases composite cost; TPC's effect is to reduce mid-training drift and tighten seed variance. The remaining components address out-of-distribution properties measured separately in the generalization study.
Without retraining, the learned policy retains its performance under route stochasticity up to $\sigma = 1.0$ (within $8\%$ of training), degrades by $25\%$ at $\sigma = 2.0$, and remains operational at $\sigma = 3.0$ with $48\%$ wait increase relative to the training distribution.

Several directions merit future investigation.
First, extending TransitDuet to multi-route networks with shared fleets and transfer coordination would test whether the hierarchical structure scales to the combinatorial action spaces of realistic transit systems.
Second, real-world deployment---potentially through a calibrated digital twin with domain randomization---would validate the framework's robustness to phenomena not captured by the current simulator, including traffic signal interactions and driver behavior heterogeneity.
Third, integrating real-time demand forecasting and modeling passenger demand elasticity would close the loop between service quality and ridership.
Finally, coupling TransitDuet with passenger information systems---broadcasting predicted arrival times based on the learned holding policy---could amplify the welfare benefits by reducing perceived waiting time alongside actual waiting time.
