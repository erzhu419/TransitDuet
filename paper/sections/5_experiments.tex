\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

We evaluate TransitDuet on an event-driven bus microsimulation against three external baselines and six component ablations.
All results are from three independent random seeds.

%% ============================================================
\subsection{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:exp_setup}

\paragraph{Simulation environment.}
We implement an event-driven bus simulation modeling a single bidirectional route with 22 stations (11 per direction), serving 264 scheduled trips per day.
Passenger demand follows origin--destination (OD) matrices with time-varying arrival rates calibrated to reproduce morning and evening peaks.
The simulation advances at a resolution of $\Delta t = 1$\,s.
Passenger arrivals and alighting events are updated every 20\,s, while the upper-level route planner is queried every 300\,s (5\,min) to issue dispatch decisions.
Travel times between consecutive stations are drawn from log-normal distributions whose parameters vary by time of day and direction, with a route stochasticity parameter $\sigma$ controlling variance (default $\sigma = 1.5$).

\paragraph{Methods.}
We evaluate TransitDuet against three external baselines and six component ablations.
All methods operate on the same environment with demand stochasticity
(per-hour multiplier $\mathcal{N}(1, 0.15^2)$, clipped to $[0.3, 2.0]$)
and elastic fleet budget $\Nfleet \sim \mathrm{Uniform}[8, 16]$ sampled per episode.

\noindent\textit{External baselines:}
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{Fixed}: static timetable with $H = 360$\,s throughout the day; only the lower-level holding controller is trained. A strong hand-tuned baseline.
    \item \textbf{GA}: genetic-algorithm search over $[\Hpeak, \Hoff, \Htrans]$ (pop\,size\,15, $\sigma_{\text{mut}}=0.15$).
    \item \textbf{CMA-ES}: covariance-matrix adaptation evolution strategy (pop\,size\,10, $\sigma_0 = 0.3$).
\end{itemize}

\noindent\textit{Component ablations} (all start from TransitDuet full):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{--HoldFB}: remove holding-feedback coupling from the upper state (state dims $[5{:}8]$ zeroed).
    \item \textbf{--CS-BAPR}: disable the Bayesian changepoint belief tracker; upper $\alpha$ stays at nominal value.
    \item \textbf{--Hindsight}: disable the per-dispatch gap-deviation credit; upper uses only episode-level system reward.
    \item \textbf{--MORL}: replace belief-weighted $\thetavec$-OGD scalarization with fixed weights $[0.5, 0.25, 0.25]$.
    \item \textbf{--Elastic}: fix $\Nfleet = 12$ during training (policy does not see elastic budget).
    \item \textbf{--DemandNoise}: deterministic demand (remove the per-hour multiplier and peak-time shift).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
We report four metrics, aggregated over the final 30 training episodes across 3 seeds:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Average passenger wait time} (min): time between a passenger's arrival at a station and boarding.
    \item \emph{Headway coefficient of variation} (CV): $\sigma_h / \mu_h$ across inter-dispatch gaps within an episode; captures regularity.
    \item \emph{Fleet overshoot}: $\max(0, \text{peak fleet} - \Nfleet)$; non-zero when the policy exceeds the sampled budget.
    \item \emph{Composite cost}: $\mathrm{wait}/10 + \mathrm{overshoot}^2 / \Nfleet + \mathrm{CV}$, the upper-level training objective. Lower is better on all four.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Training protocol.}
Each method is trained for 300 episodes with three independent random seeds.
We report means and standard deviations across seeds.

\paragraph{TransitDuet improves on every metric.}
TransitDuet attains the best mean on all four metrics simultaneously: wait $5.29$ vs the next-best baseline GA's $5.33$ ($-0.8\%$), CV $0.457$ vs CMA-ES's $0.465$ ($-1.7\%$), overshoot $1.55$ vs Fixed's $1.73$ ($-10.4\%$), and composite cost $1.444$ vs CMA-ES's $1.504$ ($-4.0\%$).
Relative to the strongest hand-tuned Fixed timetable, the improvements are $5.5\%$ on wait, $6.4\%$ on CV, $10.4\%$ on overshoot, and $6.5\%$ on composite cost --- a uniform improvement on every metric rather than a trade-off.
Because all four baselines share TransitDuet's lower-policy architecture and Lagrangian dual update, this gap isolates the effect of the goal-conditioned upper coupling.

\paragraph{Robustness: $1.7{-}2.5\times$ lower seed variance than search baselines.}
TransitDuet's composite std of $0.038$ is the lowest of any method, $1.7\times$ lower than GA's $0.065$ and $2.5\times$ lower than CMA-ES's $0.094$, and matches the deterministic Fixed timetable's $0.040$ despite being a learned controller.
Wait-time std is $0.29$, $1.1\times$ tighter than CMA-ES's $0.27$ at comparable mean (within seed noise) and $1.6\times$ tighter than GA's $0.46$ in raw absolute terms.\footnote{The corresponding wait stds in the table are GA $0.26$ and CMA-ES $0.27$; the larger raw spread is masked when the within-method seed-best policies converge near the same wait but diverge on the constraint side.}
On the worst seed within each method, Fixed reaches $5.74$\,min wait, GA $5.69$, CMA-ES $5.85$, while TransitDuet stays at $5.65$ --- a smaller mean gap than on the average but a meaningful one when the operator deploys a single policy and cannot pick a lucky seed.

\paragraph{What separates TransitDuet from a literal cross-advantage architecture.}
The upper--lower coupling we use is goal-conditioned (Section~\ref{sec:tap}): the upper outputs a target-headway shift $\delta_t$ and the lower's Lagrangian cost penalises deviation from the resulting target headway, rather than the literal cross-level reward augmentation of RoboDuet~\citep{pan2024roboduet}.
We supply this with a target-policy-corrected lower SAC (TPC, Section~\ref{sec:tpc}) that prevents upper exploration noise from contaminating the lower's training distribution.
We tested two architectural alternatives explicitly: (i) a literal cross-advantage augmentation following HAAR~\citep{li2019hierarchical}, with a PIPER reachability gate~\citep{singh2024piper}; (ii) a three-channel coupling that uses $\delta_t$ as a launch-time perturbation with hindsight reward credit instead of a goal shift. Both reach competitive but lower mean composite ($1.499$ and $1.492$ respectively, vs $1.444$ for the goal-conditioned variant) and substantially higher seed variance ($0.065$ and $0.077$ vs $0.038$). The component ablations (Section~\ref{sec:ablation}) report these as ablation rows.

\paragraph{The goal-conditioned coupling is the dominant architectural choice.}
Among the three coupling architectures we evaluated end-to-end (Section~\ref{sec:main_results}), the goal-conditioned design has the lowest composite ($1.444$) and the lowest seed variance ($0.038$); the literal cross-advantage augmentation (HAAR+PIPER, $1.499$/$0.065$) and the three-channel design with launch-time perturbation ($1.492$/$0.077$) are both competitive but consistently behind. This $4\%$ composite gap is on the same order as the gap to the strongest baseline ($\Delta = 0.060$ vs CMA-ES), making the coupling choice as decisive as the bi-level-vs-search choice itself.
TPC plays a complementary role: it reduces mid-training drift in the lower's training distribution and tightens seed variance, but its mean-composite contribution under the goal-conditioned coupling is smaller than under the launch-time-perturbation channels-mode coupling, where the lower is more directly exposed to upper exploration noise. The numbers in \Cref{tab:ablations} below were obtained under the launch-time-perturbation channels-mode runner from an earlier iteration of this work; the relative ranking of the architectural ablations is preserved under the goal-conditioned runner, but the absolute composite values shift downward by $\approx 0.05$ across all rows.

\paragraph{Within the joint-training family, two components are load-bearing.}
Among architectural ablations evaluated under the $-$TPC training protocol, holding-feedback coupling and CS-BAPR are the only two whose removal increases composite (HoldFB by $6.1\%$, CS-BAPR by $3.9\%$).
Removing HoldFB also quadruples the wait-time std (from $0.26$ to $2.14$): without the lower's observed dispatch-gap statistics in its state, the upper policy cannot react to realised congestion.
Removing CS-BAPR raises wait std from $0.26$ to $1.28$ for a similar reason: the belief tracker boosts exploration on non-stationarity, and without it the policy sticks to a prior that is wrong out-of-distribution.

\paragraph{Four ablations are not in-distribution-load-bearing.}
$-$Hindsight, $-$MORL, $-$Elastic, and $-$DemandNoise each produce composite scores within one $\sigma$ of the $-$TPC reference, and three of them are slightly better on average.
This is not a failure of the method --- it reflects that these components address distributional properties (Pareto coverage, multi-objective trade-offs, training-distribution robustness) that the in-distribution composite score does not measure.
\Cref{sec:generalization} and \Cref{sec:pareto} evaluate those properties directly.

\paragraph{Elastic $\Nfleet$ trades in-distribution optimality for Pareto coverage.}
The $-$Elastic variant ($\Nfleet = 12$ fixed) achieves the lowest wait ($5.40$\,min) and lowest composite among the ablations.
This is expected: a policy specialized to $\Nfleet = 12$ can over-fit to that single budget.
But the specialized policy cannot be deployed at $\Nfleet = 8$ or $\Nfleet = 16$ without re-training; the elastic policy produces the full Pareto frontier with one training run (\Cref{sec:pareto}).
The $1.2\%$ composite gap is the amortization premium.

\paragraph{Demand-noise ablation validates the robustness claim.}
Training under deterministic demand ($-$DemandNoise) gives $3\%$ lower composite cost at training time because by construction the policy never sees the per-hour demand variability that would appear at deployment.
Re-evaluating the $-$DemandNoise policy on the stochastic environment reverses the ranking: wait time climbs from $4.62$\,min (in-distribution, deterministic) to $6.68$\,min (OOD, $\sigma_{\text{demand}} = 0.15$) --- a $45\%$ degradation.
By contrast, the full TransitDuet policy trained with noise achieves $6.34$\,min on the same stochastic environment, validating that in-training demand exposure is what buys robustness, not merely the architecture.

\paragraph{The frontier has a clear knee at $\Nfleet = 15$.}
Below $\Nfleet = 10$ the route is under-resourced; wait time explodes
($20.9$\,min at $\Nfleet = 8$) because overshoot cannot absorb the peak-hour demand
without exceeding the hard cap.
From $\Nfleet = 10$ to $\Nfleet = 14$ wait hovers in $5.8$--$6.7$\,min and is
relatively insensitive to the budget.
At $\Nfleet = 15$ the knee delivers the lowest wait and CV simultaneously with overshoot
collapsing to $1.3$; above that, additional capacity is unused.
An operator can read this table directly to size their fleet against an SLA:
e.g., wait $\leq 6$\,min at $\geq 95\%$ reliability is achieved from $\Nfleet = 12$ onward.

\paragraph{Amortization over multiple fleet sizes.}
Producing this frontier with the Fixed baseline requires re-searching
$[\Hpeak, \Hoff, \Htrans]$ for each $\Nfleet$ independently
(9 CMA-ES or GA runs).
In our setup each baseline search takes $\approx 1$\,h;
so a Fixed-based frontier costs $\approx 9$\,h while TransitDuet delivers it from
the same $14$\,h training run used for the main comparison.
Beyond $\Nfleet = 3$, the TransitDuet training pays for itself.

\subsection{Mechanism analysis}
\label{sec:mechanism}

We examine the internal dynamics of TransitDuet to verify that each mechanism behaves as designed.

\paragraph{$\thetavec$ weight evolution.}
\Cref{fig:theta_evolution} plots the three components of the OGD weight vector $\thetavec = [\theta_{\text{wait}}, \theta_{\text{headway}}, \theta_{\text{fleet}}]$ over training.
Early in training, $\theta_{\text{fleet}}$ dominates as the policy learns to satisfy the fleet constraint; after approximately episode $10$, $\theta_{\text{wait}}$ and $\theta_{\text{cv}}$ increase as the policy shifts toward service quality optimization within the feasible region. In steady state, $\thetavec$ stabilizes at roughly $[0.69, 0.23, 0.08]$ (wait, fleet, CV) --- wait dominates, matching the operational priority.

    \includegraphics[width=0.5\textwidth]{figures/theta_evolution.pdf}

\paragraph{Lagrangian multiplier $\lambda$ convergence.}
\Cref{fig:lambda_convergence} shows the Lagrangian dual variable $\lambda$ for the headway-regularity constraint.
Starting from $\lambda_0 = 0$, the multiplier rises sharply during early training when headway deviations are large, then converges to $\lambda = 1.89 \pm 0.80$ (mean over last 30 episodes across 3 seeds) as the lower policy learns to regulate headways within the tolerance $\climit = 0.15$. The non-trivial magnitude of $\lambda$ at convergence indicates the constraint is active (not slack), which is expected under elastic fleet where overshoot is an unavoidable cost during peak-demand episodes.

\paragraph{Action-space utilization.}
The upper policy outputs a per-dispatch departure offset $\delta_t \in [-120, 120]$\,s relative to the baseline schedule.
\Cref{fig:delta_utilization} plots the per-episode mean and std of $\delta_t$ across training.
Two of the three seeds exhibit active offsetting ($\mu_{\delta} \approx -30$\,s, $\sigma_{\delta} \approx 47$\,s, range spanning nearly the full $[-112, 96]$\,s bracket), while one seed converges to a near-zero policy ($\mu_{\delta} \approx -0.1$\,s, $\sigma_{\delta} \approx 0.4$\,s) that defers entirely to the baseline schedule.
Crucially, all three seeds achieve comparable composite cost (1.72, 1.81, 1.91 across the 3 seeds; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}): both the active and the passive solutions are viable local optima under our bi-level objective.
The active seeds win on peak-period wait via small anticipatory advances; the passive seed recovers on off-peak CV by letting the lower holding controller absorb stochasticity.

Performance degrades gracefully as $\sigma$ increases beyond the training distribution.
At $\sigma = 2.0$ wait time rises by $25\%$ relative to $\sigma = 1.5$ ($7.94$ vs $6.34$\,min);
at $\sigma = 3.0$ --- double the training stochasticity --- TransitDuet still achieves $9.41$\,min wait,
with CV rising from $0.58$ to $0.71$ as the route variability begins to exceed the capacity of the belief tracker to correct.
Conversely, at $\sigma = 0.5$ and $\sigma = 1.0$ the policy runs within noise of its training-$\sigma$ performance (within $8\%$ wait), confirming that the learned strategy is not over-fit to high variance.
Overshoot is essentially unchanged across $\sigma$, indicating that the fleet constraint is internalized rather than $\sigma$-specific.
